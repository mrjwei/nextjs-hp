## Introduction

Active transport, also known as active travel, refers to transport methods including walking, cycling and the use of other non-motorised vehicles such as skateboards, kick scooters, roller skates and roller blades (iMOVE, n.d.). Despite many health-related, environmental, and economic benefits, its adoption remains low: active travel accounts for only **8%** of all trips made in Brisbane. In contrast, over **80%** of trips are made by private vehicles (Infrastructure Australia, 2019).

## Research Goal

The aim of the research was to investigate how foreign residents of Brisbane make decisions on transport methods and to gain in-depth insights into the challenges they face that block them from choosing more active transport modes.

The choice of the scope was intentional. Foreign residents are a significant demographic in Brisbane, and they often face unique challenges related to transportation, such as unfamiliarity with local infrastructure, language barriers, and cultural differences in transport habits. By focusing on this group, the research aimed to uncover specific pain points and opportunities for intervention that could encourage more active travel among foreign residents, ultimately contributing to a more sustainable and inclusive transportation ecosystem in Brisbane.

## Methodology

The research focused on qualitative methods, including in-depth interviews and contextual inquiries with foreign residents of Brisbane. Participants were recruited through community organizations and personal networks. The research process involved:

1. Identification of a theoretical framework to guide the research and analysis.
2. Interviews, combined with mapping sessions, to explore participants' transportation habits, preferences, and challenges.
3. Diary studies to capture real-time reflections on participants' transportation choices and experiences over a period of one week. This shortened period, compared to the typical two-week diary study, was chosen to reduce participant burden while still providing valuable insights into their transportation habits and decision-making processes. It was also due to the tight timeline of the project.
4. Thematic analysis of the collected data to identify key themes and insights related to transportation choices and barriers to active travel.
5. Design specification generation based on the research findings to inform design decisions that could promote active travel among foreign residents in Brisbane.

Further user interviews and testing sessions were conducted to obtain feedback on design prototypes at later stages of the project, but they are not covered in this article as the focus here is on the user research phase.

### My Role

The research process 1 to 5 was conducted by a team of three members, including myself. We shared responsibility for recruiting participants, conducting interviews and mapping sessions, analyzing the data, and generating design specifications based on the research findings.

From the prototyping stage onwards, I worked as the sole designer and developer of the project, responsible for creating design prototypes based on the research insights and iterating on them based on user feedback. The design and development process is covered in a separate article, so it is not included here.

### Theoretical Framework

The research was guided by the **Hook Model** (Eyal, 2014), which describes how products can create habit-forming behaviors through a cycle of Trigger, Action, Variable Reward, and Investment. This framework was used to analyze the factors influencing transport choices and to identify potential intervention points for promoting active travel.
